HANSEL, GRETIL, and Vertigo (Kalpataru Grove Quarterly Update 2026 Q1)

April 1, 2026

This is the first of hopefully many regular quarterly updates ranging over my personal projects and independent research.

The first quarter of 2026 was VERY eventful, basically because of Claude Code. Compared to Gemini CLI and ChatGPT which I had been relying on in months prior, Claude is astoundingly thorough, thoughtful, and efficient. A $20/mo plan has been plenty for my purposes. In early February, I took my first two big swings. First, I let it loose on Pāṇḍitya, with the instructions to revamp the UI with a sidebar and make it mobile-friendly. Two days later, I was able to use Pāṇḍitya on my phone effectively for the first time. Next, I asked Claude to duplicate my personal website hosted on Squarespace. A few days later, I had deployed the new version on my own server and canceled my pricey Squarespace subscription. Sold.

Around the same time, I posted the Kalpataru Grove blog post. The concept and site actually predated my discovery of Claude Code. In any case, it's just a simple place to tie everything together. In March, I presented the ecosystem components at UPenn’s Price Lab DH Seminar, and I also added Ko-fi as a crowdfunding mechanism. It's not that I need money to run these projects. But if the community ever wants to make them more independent from me personally, I figure some buy-in will help, both conceptually and, eventually, practically.

I'm also very excited about progress on both HANSEL and Skrutable. HANSEL added three texts, including a drama which warranted special display features, and the intake system has matured substantially, meaning future projects should be quicker and smoother. I presented a virtual demo of it at ISCLS 8 from the comfort of my bedroom in Brooklyn.

Skrutable, like Pāṇḍitya, also got a major facelift, as well as a refactor under the hood. I also thought it was long overdue to for it be more usable on the phone, and that required a multi-step process of modernization. I now find the new interface an absolute joy to use on my phone. And finally, it has a robust settings management system that will allow me to put more options in users' hands — just wait and see! I also noticed a breaking change in the Dharmamitra sandhi/compound splitting API and coordinated with their team on an update. Oh, and I finally got the automatic scheme detection working.

Looking ahead to Q2, the main thing on the back burner is researching and organizing access to online e-texts. This includes but is not limited to SETI. A new project, code name "Sāgarasaṅgama," will seek to illuminate the internal structures of GRETIL, Sanskrit Documents, Sanskrit Wikisource, and E-Bharatisampat, so that potential users can better understand what's there and whether it could be useful to them.

There are a number of projects now which operate by taking full copies of text and integrating them into a new system. DharmaNexus is far and away the most sophisticated and powerful — actually probably too intimidating for most people. Harry Spier's recently created searchable-sanskrit-library.org is more focused in its scope, being the online equivalent of downloading corpora onto one's computer and searching them with a powerful text editor like BBEdit — but it's starting to function as a repository, too, starting with the Skandapurāṇa Project materials. Ambuda is focused on production of new material with an OCR-based intake pipeline is aiming at 1,000 texts by the end of the year, and eventually "all of Sanskrit literature". Vishvas' system, which posts new content and content scraped from many other projects including E-Bharatisampat, has been around for a while, but few users make it past the interface. siva.sh seems to be looking to ingest material into its slick interface and then charge for access to it. Every few weeks, I learn of another big undertaking.

This is undoubtedly a moment of AI vertigo. Things are changing VERY fast, and dreams are becoming reality faster than we can talk to each other about what we're doing. Projects that have been only a glimmer in someone’s eye for the past decade now can suddenly erupt into reality, provided that that someone has a Claude subscription, a GitHub account, and a few hours to spare. But all the productivity tools in the world won’t get us together in a room, talking about how to coordinate efforts. My hope is that people will put things they're making online, openly, and publicize them, as quickly and energetically as they're creating them. We’ll still duplicate work, but hopefully we’ll have spent less time in each unit of work, and we’ll be able to harmonize related data across projects more quickly and easily.

I'm honestly not sure what I'll be writing about in three months from now. But hey, let's keep it up!
