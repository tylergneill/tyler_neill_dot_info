Quarterly Update: Jan–Mar 2026

April 1, 2026

This is the first quarterly update ranging over all Kalpataru Grove projects and related research.

After getting HANSEL off the ground last December, I realized people were losing track of all the different activities I'm doing. Rather than it just be "Tyler's stuff", I created a brand, "Kalpataru Grove", to help present the work under a unified banner. In March, I presented the ecosystem components at UPenn’s Price Lab DH Seminar, and a few days ago I added Ko-fi as a crowdfunding mechanism crowdfunding to the website. It's not that I need money to run these projects. But if the community ever wants to make them more independent from me personally, I figure some buy-in will help, both conceptually and, eventually, practically.

The bigger story, however, is that of Claude Code. Previously, I'd been relying on Gemini CLI and ChatGPT for my various technical work, which are pretty amazing in their own right. But Claude is in a different league now. Virtually overnight, it helped me revamp the UI of Pāṇḍitya by adding 1) a sidebar to optimize space and 2) actual mobile-friendliness. I could finally use it effectively on my phone! I also asked Claude, in as many words, to duplicate my personal website which I had hosted on Squarespace. A few days later, I had deployed the new version on my own server (which you're now looking at) and canceled my pricey Squarespace subscription. You can't argue with results like that.

Gradually, I turned my newfound powers to the Skrutable web app. Now, that too is actually enjoyable to use on the phone, and also better on desktop. More subtly, we improved how settings work, so now it's easier for me to add more user options. Along the way, I noticed that Dharmamitra sandhi/compound splitting API wasn't responding to Skrutable anymore. It turns out that the Dharmamitra team changed the shape of their outputs. Claude helped me fully repair the connection in one hour, start to finish. Oh, and I finally got automatic scheme detection working!

Of course, the newest big project, HANSEL, also made progress. ISCLS 8 happened in March, and being able to present HANSEL as a demo gave me the needed impetus to re-record the video tutorials. But as for more interesting improvements: Again with Claude's help, I added nice features for displaying drama (nāṭaka) texts, including chāyā toggling. This allowed me to add Bhagavadajjuka (thank you to Roland Steiner, Martin Straube, and Christian Ferstl) and Prabodhacandrodaya is also in the works (thanks to Matthew Kapstein). Vākyapadīyaprameyasaṃgraha also joined (thanks to Charles Li), being a case of a manuscript with much editorial markup in the text, which I address with retaining an "intermediate file" with this extra info and then removing the markup for the project editions. Building on that structure, I also added a third text: Nāgārjuna's Ratnāvalī. Some backstory is interesting here: In 2025, a young scholar in China named Phur Tsham quietly released his complete edition of Ratnāvalī — based on a newly discovered manuscript preserved at Drepung Monastery in Tibet — on Academia.edu. I got in touch, and he agreed to share with HANSEL. Even though the Sanskrit edition itself is not of the highest quality — neglect of Sanskrit metrical considerations is just one issue — I thought I should get the e-text on HANSEL as soon as possible, to increase awareness, not least because Phur Tsham's edition document includes full color plates of the manuscript. Rumor has it that at least one other scholar is working on a re-edition already. Anyway, I digress. The point is, this is right where HANSEL wants to be, in the thick of it, giving scholars a platform for their work and creatively transforming the data in ways I think can be of most help.

Taking stock and looking ahead, I feel a bit of vertigo, to be honest. I am enjoying operating in the space of Sanskrit e-text online. It's where I want to be contributing my talents, and it's a fun place to apply technology. That said, there's a somewhat bewildering amount of stuff happening, I think because so many technical people are taking advantage of AI. There are multiple projects racing ahead, and keeping up with them is no easy feat. My next big project, in prototype stage now, is going to try to toss a lasso around a few of these, helping to illuminate their structure so that potential users can better understand what's there and whether it could be useful to them. I'll also return to SETI, where I'll continue to map out the space.

For myself, meanwhile, the Kalpataru Grove concept is my refuge. Here, in my virtual workshop, I'm going to be gradually consolidating methods and best practices across projects, and I'll do my best to publicize even those patterns, as a further exercise in transparency and effective bookkeeping. As with a publicly traded company, there are pros and cons to being more exposed to scrutiny.

With Claude, dreams are now becoming reality faster than we can talk to each other about what we're doing. Projects that have been only a glimmer in someone’s eye for the past decade now can suddenly erupt into reality, provided that that someone has a Claude subscription, a GitHub account, and a few hours to spare. But all the productivity tools in the world won’t get us together in a room, talking about how to coordinate efforts. My hope is that people will put things they're making online, openly, and publicize them, as quickly and energetically as they're creating them. We’ll still duplicate work, but hopefully we’ll have spent less time in each unit of work, and we’ll be able to harmonize related data across projects more quickly and easily.

I'm honestly not sure what I'll be writing about in three months from now. But hey, let's keep it up!
